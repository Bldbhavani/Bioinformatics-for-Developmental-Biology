# What is RNA Sequencing?

RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) is a Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) technique to identify and quantify 

RNA moleculaes present in a biological sample.

Instead of studying DNA, RNA-seq studies the RNA produced from genes. Because RNA reflects which genes are actively being expressed, RNA-seq provides a snapshot of cellular activity at a specific moment.

In simple words:

DNA -> stores genetic information

RNA -> shows which genes are currently active

RNA-seq -> measures gene activity across the entire transcriptome.

---

# What is the Transcriptome?

The transcriptome is the complete collection of RNA molecules present in a cell or tissue at a particular time.

Unlike the genome, whcih remains relatively constant, the transcriptome changes continuosly depending on:

- Cell type
- Developmental stage
- Disease condition
- Environmental signals
- Drug treatment.

For example:

A skin cell and a neuron contain almost identical DNA, but their transcriptomes are very different because different genes are active.

---

# Why do we perform RNA Sequencing?

RNA-seq helps researchers understand:

- Which genes are expressed.
- How strongly each gene is expressed.
- Which gene change under different biological conditions.
- How diseases alter gene expression.
- How cells respond to treatments.

Instead of studying one gene at a time, RNA-seq measures thousands of genes simultaneously.

---

# Key Applications

RNA-seq is commonly used for:

- Differential gene expression analysis
- Biomarker discovery
- Alternatice splicing analysis
- Novel transcript discovery
- Gene fusion detection
- Disease mechanism studies
- Drug response analysis
- Developmental biology research

---

# Summary

RNA sequencing allows researchers to measure gene expression across the entire transcriptome. It is one of the most powerful tools in modern molecular biology because it helps explain how cells function, respond to stimuli, and change during development or disease.

# Developmental Biology Connection

RNA sequencing is widely used in developmental biology because gene expression changes continuosly as an embryo develops.

Example Include:

- Identifying genes active during early embryonic development.
- Comparing healthy and abnormal embryo development.
- Understanding cell differentiation.
- Studing implantation and placental development.
- Investigating congenital disorders.

By comparing transcriptomes at different developmental stages, researchers can understand  how cells acquire specialized function and how developmental processes are regulated.
