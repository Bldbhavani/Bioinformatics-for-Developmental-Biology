# RNA-seq Workflow

## Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

- Understand the complete RNA-seq workflow.
- Explain the purpose of each analysis step.
- Know the input and output of every stage.
- Recognize where different bioinformatics tools are used.
- Understand how raw sequencing data become biological knowledge.

---

# What is the RNA-seq workflow?

RNA sequencing is not a single experiment.

It is a series of laboratory and computational steps that convert RNA molecules from a biological sample into biological insights.

The workflow consists of two major parts:

1. Wet Lab (Experimental)
2. Bioinformatics Analysis (Computational)

Both are equally important.

# 1. Biological Sample

Every RNA-seq experiment begins with a biological sample.

Example include:

- Embryos
- Stem cells
- Placenta
- Endometrium
- Blood
- Tumor tissue
- Brain tissue
- Cell cultures

The quantity of the biological sample directly affects the quality of the sequencing results.

---

# 2. RNA Extraction

RNA is isolated from the biological sample.

The extracted RNA should be:

- High quality
- Free from contamination
- Intact (not degraded)

Poor-quality RNA leads to unreliable sequencing results.

---

# 3. Library Preparation

RNA molecules cannot be sequenced directly.

During library preparation:

- RNA is converted into complementary DNA (cDNA).
- Sequencing adapters are added.
- Fragments of suitable length are prepared.

The final product is called the sequencing library.

---

# 4. RNA Sequencing

The prepared library is loaded onto a Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) platform.

Examples:

- Ilumina
- Oxford Nanopore
- PacBio

The sequencing machine reads millions of fragments simultaneously.

The output generated by the sequencing instrument is usually stored as FASTQ files.

---

# 5. FASTQ Files

FASTQ files contain:

- The nucleotide sequence
- A quality score for every nucleotide

These files represent the raw sequencing data.

Before any biological analysis, the quality of these files must be assessed.

---

# 6. The first computational step is quality assessment.

The first computational step is quality assessment.

The most commonly used tool is:

FASTQC

Quality control checks:

- Read quality
- GC content
- Adapter containation
- Sequence duplication
- Overrepresented sequences

If problems are detected, they should be corrected before procedding.

---

# 7. Read Trimming

Low-quality basess and sequening adapters are removed.

Common trimming tools include:

- Trimmomatic
- Cutadapt
- fastp

The goal is to improve the overall quality of the sequencing reads.

---

# 8. Alignment

The cleaned sequencing reads are aligned to a reference genome.

Popular aligners include:

- STAR
- HISAT2

The output is usually stored as BAM files.

# 9. Gene Quantification

After alignment the number of reads mapping to each gene is counted.

Common tools:

- HTSeq
- featureCounts

The output is called the count matrix.

This matrix is the starting point for gene expression analysis.

# 10. Normalization

Different samples often contain different sequencing depths.

Normalization removes these technical differences.

Common normalizaion methods include.

- TPM
- CPM
- DESeq2 normalization
- EdgeR normalization

This ensures that gene expression values can be compared fairly between samples.

# 11. Differential Gene Expression Analysis

The expression level of genes are compared between biological conditions.

Examples:

Healthy embryos vs Miscarriage samples

Control vs Treatment

Disease vs Healthy tissue

Genes can be classified as:

- Upregulated
- Downregulated

These genes may explain biological differences between the conditions.

---

# 12. Biological Interpretation

The final step is to interpret the biological meaning of the results.

Researchers ask question such as:

- Which biological pathways changed?
- Which cellular processes are affected?
- Which genes may serve as biomarkers?
- Which genes could become therapeutic targets?

This step converts computational results into biological knowledge.

---

# Developmental Biology Connection

RNA-seq is widely used to study embryo development because gene expression changes continuosly thoroughout development.

Examples include:

- Comparing Day 3 and Day 5 embryos.
- Identifying genes involved in implantation.
- Investigating recurrent miscarriage.
- Studying trophoblast differentiation.
- Understanding early lineage specification.
- Exploring molecular mechanisms underlying infertility.

RNA-seq allows researchers to identify which genes are activated or supressed during these devemental processes, providing insights into normal and abnormal embryonic development.
